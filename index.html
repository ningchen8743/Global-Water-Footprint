How long will water be the most abundant resource on the planet?

Water is vital for all aspects of life, but human activity and population growth are depleting the amount of water resources available.

How did water go from ubiquitous resource to a global crisis? Data from the UN (Aquastat) and from water researchers (Hoekstra et al) show patterns of our water consumption footprints during 1996-2005.

The story of water is complex. But it's up to us to understand how water is used around the world in order to know how we might increase efficiency, safety, and sustainability for continued life on earth.

Similar to the carbon footprint concept, a water footprint is defined as the total volume of freshwater used, directly and indirectly, to produce the goods and services consumed. Water footprints of consumption serve as one important indicator to measure and understand world water use.

A global overview

We are consuming water unsustainably, at even twice the rate of population growth.

North America, with only 3 countries, accounts for 12% of the world's total consumption footprint, whereas Asia, with 31 countries, accounts for 43%.

The US has the second highest footprint of water consumption per capita. Only the United Arab Emirates consumes more, because of its dry climate with low water resources and its high volume of water consumption.

Only sub-regions in Africa and Central America fall below the world average water footprint of 1,385 cubic meters of water used per person per year. Central Africa (1,092 m3/y) has the lowest average consumption, even though this region is rich in water resources. The regions with less than average consumption are also regions with the lowest UN rating of the Human Development Index, a statistical composite index for human development potential.

A deep dive into water consumption

Beyond how much water is used, take a look at how water is being consumed across sectors.

The factors that make up a country's water consumption include the sector where that water is used -- agricultural, industrial, and domestic. We can also look at the different sources of water these sectors use, shown on the visualization by the curved lines colored in green, blue, and grey.

"Green" water, which falls as rainwater, is held in the soil and is used to grow crops. "Blue" water is fresh surface and groundwater, found in lakes, rivers, and aquifers. "Grey" water is the amount of freshwater required to dilute pollutants enough for safe consumption.

"Internal" water refers to water resources used inside a country to produce its goods and services. "External" water refers to water used for the production of goods and services that are imported into and consumed within a country.

Some facts of water use by sector:

Agriculture accounts for 70% of global water withdrawal. Excluding livestock, rice is one of the largest water consumers in the world - using 21% of the total volume of water used for crop production.

Industrial water consumption affects communities locally and internationally through trade. Apparel and footwear companies use a lot of water for the manufacturing of fabric and leather.

Domestic water consumption covers a wide variety of health, hygiene, and recreational purposes. Though it contributes relatively little to total water consumption, the kitchen source is the major contributor for most pollutants, and therefore, the amount of grey water associated with this sector.

Australia matches the US's use of water for internal agriculture, at rates highest among global rates. The country doesn't import as much food as the US, so the footprint of external agriculture is low. Australia's geographic isolation may keep imports low.

There's a stark contrast in consumption per capita for industrial purposes, as the US consumes more than twice as much water for industry. The US also consumes almost twice as much water for domestic usage.

Germany is a highly developed country and its water footprint is below the US.

Germany's agricultural water footprint reveals that two-thirds is attributed to imported food, and one-third by growing its own agriculture. This pattern of imported food is common for many neighboring European countries.

Germany's use of water in domestic households and also industry has declined in recent years, suggesting that the country undertakes successful water conservation on a per capita basis. Domestic consumption of water in Germany is one-fourth of the US rate, and internal industrial water consumption is one-tenth of the US rate.

China has the most people of any country and a pattern of per capita water consumption different than the US. The country grows most of its own food and has a much lower per capita internal agricultural water footprint than the US.

China's footprint in this sector is lower because the US has a very high consumption of meat, especially bovine meat that requires a lot of agricultural water. A hamburger has one of the highest water footprints of any food -- and in general, Americans eat much more meat than people in China.

China imports less agricultural and industrial products than the US, as reflected by the low internal consumption of water in these sectors.

Democratic Republic of the Congo has the lowest water footprint in the world. All of its consumption is low in every sector and almost non-existent for industrial use.

The 10 lowest GDPs in the world are in regions of Africa, and this country in Central Africa is among them.

While development is low, this country has vast renewable water resources. Even if water is plentiful, 49% of people here do not have access to safe drinking water, according to UN Aquastat data.

Democratic Republic of Congo has a high population growth, and its agricultural use of water per capita will be multiplied by a population that needs to eat.

The last drop

Human populations can't develop without consuming water for their health, wellbeing, and prosperity.

Water crisis under the surface

As countries grow their prosperity through GDP and their populations grow larger, these trends come with the cost of jeopardizing renewable water resources faster than they can be replenished through natural cycles. Many countries are on a path to running out completely within the next 75 years.

Water awareness is the first step. What will you be able to do? You can become involved in deciding how you and your community, and your country, make best use of water resources. The biggest footprint is in agriculture. What do you eat? Where is it grown? When we know the hidden water costs of producing the goods and services we use, we can help decide the future of the industries that build economies.
